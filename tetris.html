Video Game Facts

Tetris

Surprising History and Origins

  • It was built on a text terminal: The original, first-ever version of Tetris on the Soviet Electronika 60 computer had no graphics. Pajitnov had to use brackets and spaces to represent the falling blocks.
  • The USSR owned the rights: Because Pajitnov worked for the Soviet government, the state held the commercial rights to Tetris initially, meaning he didn't make any money from the game until he moved to the United States and founded The Tetris Company in 1996.
  • Space Game Boy: In 1993, Russian cosmonaut Aleksandr Serebrov brought a Game Boy loaded with Tetris aboard the Soyuz TM-17 spacecraft, making it the first video game ever played in outer space.

Strange Effects on the Human Brain

  • The "Tetris Effect": If you play the game long enough, your brain gets so accustomed to arranging shapes that you might start seeing falling blocks in your dreams or when you close your eyes. This documented phenomenon is officially recognized by psychologists.
  • It reduces cravings and trauma: Psychological research shows that playing Tetris can interfere with the visual imagery in your brain, helping to reduce cravings for food or cigarettes. It has also been clinically studied as a method to prevent the brain from cementing traumatic memories and lessen symptoms of PTSD.

Pop Culture & Music

  • A Russian Love Song: The game's famous chiptune theme song, heard endlessly on the Game Boy, is actually a 19th-century Russian folk tune called "Korobeiniki," which is about a peddler trying to sell his wares to a girl.
  • The Game Boy Bundle: Nintendo bundled Tetris with their original 1989 Game Boy, a bold move at the time that helped legitimize portable gaming and expanded the demographic of gamers across the globe.
